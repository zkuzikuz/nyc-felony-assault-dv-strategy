NYC has a sustained felony-assault crisis with a large domestic-violence component. The city's response isn't scaling.

The city says one number; the truth says another. Both are true. Here is what we have refused to see — and what we are being asked to do about it.

By Zarren Kuzma · June 2026

There is a number the city likes to say, and there is the truth, and they are not the same — though both, I want to insist, are true. The city can tell you that felony assault has risen seventy-two percent since 2010, and it has. But a third of that rise is a change in the bookkeeping: a 2010 law that gave an old cruelty a new name, so that the same act now counts where once it did not. Strip the paperwork away and the real increase — the rise in what people actually do to one another — is closer to forty-seven percent. I dwell on this not to make the number smaller but to make it honest, for a city that cannot count honestly cannot grieve honestly, and a city that will not grieve will not act.

Look, then, at what is rising. Murder climbed in the plague years and has come down again; robbery holds steady, and so does rape. Only assault keeps climbing, year upon year — the one wound that will not close. This is not the general weather of a hard town. It is a particular thing, and particular things have causes, and causes, if we have the stomach to name them, can be met.

And here is the part we would rather not see. The danger is not, for the most part, the stranger on the platform or the figure at the edge of the streetlight. It is the person across the kitchen table. Domestic violence is the largest and the fastest-growing piece of this rise: in 2025, nearly two of every five felony assaults in this city were counted as domestic, and domestic and elder assault together have grown by some seventy-three percent since 2017. We have built our whole idea of safety around the locked door — and for too many, the locked door is precisely where the danger lives. The home, the very word we keep for refuge, has become the most dangerous room thousands of New Yorkers will ever enter.

What has the city done with this knowledge? It has, in the main, declined to hold it. Money is a form of attention, and by that measure the city has looked away: in real dollars, spending on domestic-violence prevention has not moved in seven years, while the need has climbed — so that per person harmed, the city now spends less than it once did. Three years running, the Council asked for more: for housing, for lawyers, for shelter. Three years running, the mayor's budget answered with a zero. A zero is not an oversight. It is a sentence, passed quietly, on people whose names will never reach the papers.

I have no patience for despair; despair is a luxury, and the women in those households cannot afford it, so neither can we. There is, in fact, a plan already written and already paid for — six hundred and fifty million dollars, announced, for roughly a thousand beds, a place for the one who flees and the one in crisis to go.

The beds are mostly still on paper. Build them; fill them; put the survivor first in line. To the call that is a breakdown and not a crime, send help that does not arrive holding a gun — and send it at three in the morning, not only at noon. And where we do not yet know what works, try, in a handful of precincts, the few things the evidence will actually bear — a danger-screen at the scene, a hard and focused word to the men most likely to kill — and promise, in writing, to end whatever fails.

None of this is beyond us. The cost is a rounding error against the city's own budget gap; the obstacle has never been money, and it has never quite been knowledge. It is attention, which is only another word for love. What a city chooses to count tells you what it has agreed to value; what it refuses to count tells you what it has decided it can live without seeing. The arithmetic in these pages is, finally, an argument that we have decided to live without seeing the people in the greatest danger from the people closest to them. I am asking the city to look. To look is not yet to have saved anyone. But nothing has ever been saved that was not first looked at, full in the face.

Sources & method

Felony-assault counts are from NYPD Complaint Data Historic; the +47% behavioral figure removes the 2010 Strangulation 1st reclassification (NYPL §121.13). The domestic-violence share comes from the NY State DCJS feed via Vital City — NYPD's public data has no victim-relationship field — where the ~73% growth combines domestic and elder assault and the ~39% share is a 2025 figure. Spending is from NYC Council Finance Committee HRA reports, CPI-adjusted. The national comparison uses FBI UCR and the Council on Criminal Justice 24-city update. Program evidence: Koppa 2024 (Lethality Assessment Program) and Sechrist & Weil 2018 (offender-focused deterrence); B-HEARD figures from the NYC Comptroller's 2025 audit; the housing plan from the Mayor's January 2025 announcement.
