# Round 3 Research Findings

## Q1 — FY26 budget capacity for $40-65M new annual spending

**Gap size.** The NYC Comptroller's office projects a FY26 budget gap of roughly $4.22B (3.6% of revenues), growing to $8.83B in FY27, $9.95B in FY28, $8.54B in FY29 [Comptroller, Comments on FY26 Adopted Budget, https://comptroller.nyc.gov/reports/comments-on-new-york-citys-fiscal-year-2026-adopted-budget/]. NY State Comptroller DiNapoli flags structural risk that outyear gaps could reach ~$13.6B by FY29 [https://www.osc.ny.gov/press/releases/2025/12/dinapoli-nyc-facing-larger-budget-gaps-amid-slowing-economic-growth-and-rising-costs]. NYC's FY26 operating budget relies on $7.4B in federal funding (6.4% of total spending), with at least $535M already at risk from federal program cuts [FPWA, https://www.fpwa.org/fy-2026-nyc-budget-analysis/].

**PEG status.** The FY26 Adopted Budget achieved $1.9B in citywide savings without a formal PEG program; the Council secured restorations to prior PEGs, including $104.5M baselined for older-adult services and $94.5M for CUNY [NYC Council, https://council.nyc.gov/press/2025/06/30/2915/; Mayor's Office, https://www.nyc.gov/mayors-office/news/2025/05/mayor-adams-releases-best-budget-ever-fiscal-year-2026-executive-budget-makes-significant]. No published candidate-program reduction list specifically eligible to absorb $40-65M in new public-safety pilots was identified.

**Pilot funding-source pattern.** CMS/Cure Violence transitioned from MOCJ to DYCD in FY24/FY25; FY26 MOCJ budget is $844.9M [NYC Council, https://council.nyc.gov/press/2025/05/29/2889/]. CMS hit a record $86M in FY24 [https://www.nyc.gov/office-of-the-mayor/news/462-23/]. Comptroller's "Cure for Crisis" report documents that CMS providers receive city contract dollars supplemented by philanthropic funding (Robert Wood Johnson Foundation funded the John Jay evaluation) [https://comptroller.nyc.gov/reports/the-cure-for-crisis/]. Public-safety pilots have historically launched with mixed MOCJ discretionary, DYCD, and federal pass-through funding.

**Confidence:** medium. **Verdict:** the evidence here is inconclusive about whether $40-65M can be absorbed without displacement — the gap exists ($4.2B FY26) and no published program-reduction menu identifies absorbable lines, but $40-65M is small (~0.05%) relative to $115.9B total budget. Displacement risk is real but unspecified.

## Q2 — NYC pilot-termination track record

No aggregate accounting of NYC pilot terminations following negative evaluations was located in published comptroller, IBO, or academic sources. Case-by-case evidence is mixed:

- **B-HEARD**: Comptroller Levine's 2024 audit found 35% of eligible mental-health calls (13,042) received no B-HEARD response with no documented reason, plus 14,200 missed overnight calls; the program remains stuck at 31 of 77 precincts with ~9-18 teams. Despite negative audit findings, B-HEARD was not terminated; advocacy continues for expansion [https://comptroller.nyc.gov/newsroom/new-audit-comptroller-finds-over-a-third-of-eligible-mental-health-calls-did-not-get-a-b-heard-team-response-for-untracked-reasons/; https://www.nylpi.org/op-ed-need-heard-expand-nycs-successful-b-heard-teams-by-the-new-york-daily-news-editorial-review-board/].
- **Cure Violence/CMS**: John Jay REC's 2015-2017 evaluations produced mixed/positive findings (18% homicide drop in CV areas 2010-13; attitudinal shifts), but Comptroller's 2024 "Cure for Crisis" found "rampant delays," lack of outcome tracking, and management deficiencies — yet CMS funding rose to $86M and continues [https://johnjayrec.nyc/2015/01/16/researchbrief201501/; https://comptroller.nyc.gov/reports/the-cure-for-crisis/].
- **NYPD predictive policing**: NYPD evaluated PredPol/Azavea/Keystats in 2016, declined commercial procurement, then built Patternizr internally; Patternizr reportedly continues in use through 2025 despite bias critiques [https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/nypd-predictive-policing-documents; Fordham ULJ, https://ir.lawnet.fordham.edu/ulj/vol47/iss1/2/].
- **SCOUT (Subway Co-Response)**: Launched Oct 2023; expanded from 2 to 10 teams via $20M in Hochul/Adams funding without published outcome evaluation [https://www.nyc.gov/mayors-office/news/2024/10/mayor-adams-new-co-response-operation-focused-serving-more-new-yorkers-need-on; https://www.governor.ny.gov/news/safer-subways-governor-hochul-announces-subway-crime-track-reach-lowest-levels-generation-2025].

**Confidence:** medium. **Verdict:** the evidence here would support the R3 critique's premise — among NYC public-safety pilots launched 2010-2024 with announced "rigorous evaluation" components, the locatable cases (B-HEARD, CMS, Patternizr, SCOUT) all continued or expanded despite mixed/negative/absent evaluation findings. No aggregate kill-switch accounting exists, so the verdict is directional rather than statistical.

## Q3 — LAP deployment effects on moderate-risk service access

**Oklahoma evaluation (Messing et al. 2014/2015).** The Oklahoma quasi-experimental study at 7-month follow-up found LAP-intervention survivors were **more** likely to access DV services than controls (31.2% vs 20.4%), used more protective strategies, and reported less subsequent physical violence [https://crimesolutions.ojp.gov/ratedprograms/lethality-assessment-program-oklahoma; https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/doi/10.1086/683194]. The Lethality Screen has 92-93% sensitivity and 93-96% negative predictive value, designed to minimize false negatives [Messing et al., https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25969441/].

**Maryland and other rollouts.** Published evaluations of Maryland LAP and MNADV implementations report increased help-seeking and shelter contact among screened survivors; no published evaluation located documents reduced advocate-referral or shelter-bed access for self-identified survivors below the lethality cutoff [https://www.mnadv.org/wp-content/uploads/2021/02/LAP-Effectiveness-Position-Paper.pdf; https://vawnet.org/material/lap-info-packet-and-faq]. A ScienceDirect study of an information-focused police IPV intervention found homicide-reduction effects [https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0167268123004018].

**Service-rationing/gating critique.** No peer-reviewed empirical study located that measures whether LAP adoption reduces service access for sub-cutoff (low-risk) survivors. General street-level-bureaucracy literature documents service rationing in 37% of police-discretion studies [https://academic.oup.com/jpart/article/35/4/397/8198029], but this is not LAP-specific. The published LAP design pattern explicitly screens **in** high-risk cases for hotline connection; the protocol does not prescribe denying services to low-risk survivors, but neither does it prescribe outreach for them — moderate-risk service-access metrics are largely absent from published evaluations.

**Confidence:** medium. **Verdict:** the evidence here is inconclusive about the rationing claim. Published LAP evaluations measure high-risk help-seeking gains (which are positive) but do not report on low-risk/moderate-risk service access; the absence-of-evidence is not evidence-of-absence. No published design pattern explicitly prevents LAP from being used as a rationing key, but no evaluation documents this happening either.

## Q4 — Named-TA-partner procurement pattern

**NNSC sole-source TA.** NNSC is housed at John Jay/CUNY. Where cities adopt GVI with NNSC as TA partner, the TA contract itself is typically sole-sourced based on NNSC's "unique qualifications" — e.g., Minneapolis FY25 awarded a sole-source contract to Research Foundation of CUNY/NNSC for GVI TA [https://lims.minneapolismn.gov/RCA/25322]. Implementation contracts (the local CBO providers) are separate procurements; no published evidence located that NNSC's TA role mechanically pre-determines the CBO vendor pool, though NNSC's strategic-advising role shapes implementation design.

**CMS vendor-pool composition.** Comptroller analyzed ~1,400 payments across 112 contracts totaling $192M for CMS providers FY16-FY25 [https://comptroller.nyc.gov/reports/the-cure-for-crisis/]. Named CMS providers include Man Up! Inc. (founded pre-CMS, funded 2012), Save Our Streets/SOS Bronx (Center for Court Innovation spinoff), Elite Learners (Brownsville), King of Kings Foundation, BronxConnect — most pre-existed Cure Violence/CMS as community organizations rather than being NNSC/John Jay spinoffs [https://www.elitelearners.org/post/working-with-community-citys-crisis-management-system-continues-work-to-reducing-pandemic-era-spike-in-gun-violence; https://kingofkingsfoundation.org/crisis-management/; https://bronxconnect.org/programs/]. The full 24-vendor breakdown by founding provenance was not located in published form.

**Culturally-specific DV CBO contract scale.** Sakhi, Womankind, Korean American Family Service Center, Sauti Yetu, and Violence Intervention Program form "The Collective"; advocacy materials state these culturally-specific organizations remain "among the most underfunded" relative to need [https://www.iamwomankind.org/news-and-updates/womankind-advocates-for-new-york-states-investment-in-culturally-specific-gender-based-violence-services/]. No published comparison locates these organizations holding city contracts at CMS-vendor scale ($1-10M+ annually).

**Procurement set-asides.** NYC Procurement Policy Board rules include MWBE preferences (NYC awards ~5% / $2.4B of $20B procurement to MWBEs [https://comptroller.nyc.gov/newsroom/city-contracting-practices-leave-minority-and-women-owned-businesses-m-wbes-behind/]) and PPB Rule §1-02 exempts line-item discretionary awards to community-based not-for-profits from competitive source-selection [https://codelibrary.amlegal.com/codes/newyorkcity/latest/NYCrules/0-0-0-21089]. No NYC procurement set-aside specifically for "culturally-specific" CBOs was located.

**Confidence:** low-medium. **Verdict:** the evidence here is inconclusive about disproportionate awards to NNSC's network. NNSC TA contracts are sole-sourced, but published CMS vendor data shows the implementation pool is dominated by pre-existing community CBOs rather than NNSC/John Jay spinoffs. No NYC set-aside specifically protects culturally-specific DV CBOs from being crowded out, and these CBOs are documented as comparatively under-resourced — but the causal link from "named TA partner" to "vendor-pool exclusion" is not empirically established in available sources.
