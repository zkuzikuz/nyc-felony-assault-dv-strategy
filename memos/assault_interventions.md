# Felony Assault in NYC: Drivers, Interventions, and the Evidence Gap

*Research brief supporting strategy memo on NYC felony-assault rise (2010-2024).*
*Date: 2026-04-29.*

---

## 1. Likely drivers of the 2010-2024 rise

The most defensible drivers, ranked by evidence quality:

**A. Domestic / household violence (strongest evidence).** Vital City's analysis of NYPD data finds that domestic and elderly assaults combined rose 73% from 2017 to 2024, vs. 40% for other felony assaults — i.e., DV is a disproportionate share of the increase. Roughly 40% of NYC felony assaults involve members of the same household. Felony strangulations alone rose ~60% from 2017 to 2024 (to ~4,500 cases), reflecting both real escalation and a 2010 NY law that elevated strangulation to a felony charge — a partial reporting-threshold artifact, but only a partial one.

**B. Untreated serious mental illness + supportive-housing gap (strong, partly indirect).** NY State lost >700 inpatient psychiatric beds 2014-2021 (Manhattan Institute / Vital City). An estimated 2,000 New Yorkers with serious mental illness cycle between streets, subways, jails, and hospitals. NYPD and Vital City both flag untreated behavioral-health crises as a plausible component of the "stranger" felony-assault category, though direct attribution is hard.

**C. Reporting / classification shifts (real but bounded).** The 2010 strangulation statute, expanded DV-coding, and 2015+ NYPD victim-friendlier intake practices all push counts up without proportional incident growth. These explain *some* but not the majority of the rise — the trend is monotonic and broad-based across sub-categories.

**Drivers commonly cited but NOT well-supported as primary causes:** Bail reform (peer-reviewed synthetic-control studies — Bondurant et al., U-Albany — find negligible effects on violent-crime trends); stop-and-frisk rollback (timing mismatches the 2010-onset trend). Subway assault is salient but a small share of the citywide felony-assault total.

## 2. What NYC has tried (named programs, scope, evidence)

| Program | What it does | Scope | Evidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| **B-HEARD** (Behavioral Health Emergency Assistance Response Division) | EMS-led, non-police 911 response to mental-health calls | FY24: 14,900 calls in pilot precincts (~28 of 77); 73% of eligible calls in operating hours | Comptroller audit (2024): 35% of *eligible* calls still didn't get a B-HEARD team, reasons untracked. Quasi-experimental study in *Psychiatric Services* (2025) shows reduced unnecessary ED transports. **Promising but limited geographic coverage.** |
| **Crisis Management System (CMS) / Cure Violence** | 20+ community violence interrupter (CVI) sites, hospital responder teams, conflict mediation | $86M FY24 (up from $4.8M at start); 29 of 77 precincts; managed via Office of Neighborhood Safety | Comptroller Lander 2024 report: **21% reduction in shootings** in CVI areas, ~1,567 fewer shootings 2012-2024. John Jay REC (2017) found 50% gun-injury drop in East NY Cure Violence area, 37% in S. Bronx. **Strongest NYC-specific evidence base — but for shootings, not felony assault broadly.** Operational issues: payment delays now 255 days. |
| **NYPD Subway Safety Task Force / Community Response Team** | +1,000 officers/day surged into transit (Feb 2024); CRT quality-of-life unit | Citywide subway; CRT formed 2022 with 18 officers | Subway crime down ~3% 2022→2023 and further in 2024 — but no published causal evaluation. NYPD IG (2024) found CRT operating without written policies/training. **Tactical, not evidence-graded.** |
| **NYC Ceasefire (Group Violence Intervention)** | Focused-deterrence call-ins to gang-involved individuals; Brooklyn launch Dec 2014, expanded Bronx + SI | Limited footprint, run with NNSC at John Jay | Boston Ceasefire reduced youth gun homicide ~63%; Campbell Collaboration meta-analysis confirms strong effects. **NYC-specific outcome evaluation thin** — John Jay REC has compared but not rigorously evaluated NYC Ceasefire impact. |
| **Bridge to Home / Care, Community, Action** | $650M plan; 100 transitional psych-housing beds with on-site clinical care + 900 Safe Haven beds | Announced Jan 2025; first facility opened Sept 2025 ($13M annual baseline) | Too new for outcome data. **Promising design, sub-scale relative to ~2,000-person target population.** |
| **PATH (Partnership Assistance for Transit Homelessness)** | Co-response NYPD + DHS + H+H clinical outreach in subways | Citywide subway, launched 2024 | 11,000 engagements, 3,000 services delivered (admin metrics, not impact eval). |

## 3. Evidence-based interventions: what works for felony assault

Ranked by research quality, *for assault/violence reduction specifically*:

1. **Hot-spots policing with problem-oriented design** — Braga et al. Campbell Collaboration meta-analysis: **29% reduction in assault**, 36% in firearm crime, with no displacement. Strongest single evidence base for *assault* outcome.
2. **Focused deterrence / Group Violence Intervention** — Campbell systematic review: "strong empirical evidence." Boston Ceasefire: 63-68% reduction in youth gun violence. Effects concentrated on group-involved violence; weaker fit for DV-driven felony assault.
3. **Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for at-risk youth (Becoming a Man model)** — Heller et al. RCTs in Chicago: **45% reduction in violent-crime arrests**, 19% increase in graduation; benefit-cost ratio 5-30x. Replicable, scalable.
4. **Hospital-Based Violence Intervention Programs (HVIPs)** — Mixed RCT evidence; recent systematic reviews (2024-2025) show recidivism + psychosocial gains, but RCTs underpowered. NYC's hospital-responder CVI work overlaps this.
5. **CAHOOTS-style mobile crisis response** — NBER (Shem-Tov 2024): expansion reduced arrests, increased medical access; ~5-8% true police-call diversion. Effects on *assault* itself are indirect.
6. **Built-environment / lighting** — Moderate evidence (Chalfin et al. NYCHA lighting RCT showed crime reductions); not assault-specific.

## 4. The actionable gap

**NYC funds CMS at ~$86M for shootings — but has no equivalently scaled, evaluated program targeting domestic-violence felony assault, which now drives the majority of the categorical rise.** The DV system is service-fragmented (ENDGBV, family justice centers, hotlines) and lacks the precinct-targeted, outcome-evaluated, dollar-attached structure of CMS.

**Strategy-memo prescription:** *Stand up a "DV Crisis Management System" — a $40-60M, evaluation-built program that ports the CMS architecture (precinct-level CVI sites, 24/7 responders, John Jay REC outcome evaluation) onto domestic and household violence. Pair it with mandated B-HEARD expansion to all 77 precincts (currently 28) and a doubled Bridge to Home bed count. This combines the strongest NYC-proven implementation model (CMS) with the strongest demand signal (DV is 40%+ of felony assault and 73% of the categorical growth) — neither of which CompStat-style aggregate management surfaces.*
