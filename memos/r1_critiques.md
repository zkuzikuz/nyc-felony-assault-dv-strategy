# Round 1 Critiques — Skeptical Hiring Manager

## Critique 1: The DV thesis rests almost entirely on a single secondary source the author did not re-derive

- **Severity:** high
- **Specific memo passage attacked:** "Vital City's analysis of NYPD data finds **domestic and elder assault rose 73% from 2017 to 2024 vs 40% for other felony assault**, and ~40% of NYC felony assaults involve members of the same household. **Domestic violence is the dominant driver** of the categorical rise."
- **Why this is a problem:** The entire load-bearing claim of the memo — that DV drives the rise and therefore DV deserves a CMS port — is a single citation to one Vital City article (echoed by Gothamist, which cites the same data). The author's own data caveat admits "we cannot distinguish intimate-partner from street from transit assault" inside pd_cd 109. The thesis cannot be stronger than its weakest link, and the weakest link is an un-replicated third-party number sitting on top of NYPD micro-data the author chose not to pull. If Vital City's 73% figure includes misdemeanor DV, uses a different denominator, or counts elder-assault complaints with mixed relationship coding, the entire pyramid collapses.
- **Suggested research to confirm or deny:** Test whether the 73%/40% DV-vs-non-DV split reproduces from NYPD's victim-relationship fields in the underlying complaint data, or from DCJS/ENDGBV intimate-partner aggregates, using the same 2017–2024 window Vital City used.

## Critique 2: The "+47% behavioral rise" headline is itself partly statutory and partly classification drift

- **Severity:** high
- **Specific memo passage attacked:** "The +72% headline overstates real behavioral change because the 2010 anti-strangulation statute … contributed ~35% of the 2010→2024 absolute increase … **The honest behavioral rise is +47%** — still substantial, still pre-dating COVID, still invisible in NYC's institutional reporting."
- **Why this is a problem:** The memo treats +47% as a clean behavioral residual, but the supporting `assault_composition.md` flags three additional new sub-categories (Assault Other Public Service Employee, Traffic Agent, School Safety Agent) that did not exist in 2010, plus the assault_interventions brief notes "expanded DV-coding and 2015+ NYPD victim-friendlier intake practices all push counts up without proportional incident growth." The strangulation adjustment removes one named statutory event; it does not adjust for the others or for the broader intake/coding shift the author concedes exists. Calling +47% "behavioral" is a category error — it's "what's left after one statutory adjustment," not a clean behavioral rate.
- **Suggested research to confirm or deny:** Determine whether NYC felony-assault victimization rates from independent sources (NCVS NYC oversample, hospital ED assault-injury counts, DCJS arrest data) move at a rate consistent with the +47% complaint-data figure over 2010–2024.

## Critique 3: The clustering is doing rhetorical work it cannot statistically support

- **Severity:** medium
- **Specific memo passage attacked:** "clustering all 16 top felony categories on their 2017-2024 trajectories yields three statistically supported clusters (silhouette = 0.44 at k=3)"
- **Why this is a problem:** Silhouette = 0.44 is "weak structure that could be artificial" by the conventional Kaufman/Rousseeuw rubric (>0.5 is reasonable, >0.7 is strong). The memo presents the three clusters as if they're robust groupings; they're not. Worse, inspection of `cluster_assignments.csv` shows GRAND LARCENY and ROBBERY both land in the "Surge" cluster despite the author's own pre-COVID baseline check classifying them as declining 2010s success stories now in incomplete rebound — a known internal contradiction the memo does not acknowledge. The author also does not report whether k=2 or k=4 produce better silhouettes, nor whether the assignments are stable to bootstrap resampling.
- **Suggested research to confirm or deny:** Test whether the k=3 partition is stable under (a) varying k from 2 to 6, (b) bootstrap resampling of the offense-year matrix, and (c) substitution of distance metric (euclidean vs DTW), and report the silhouette curve rather than a single point.

## Critique 4: "Porting CMS architecture to DV" is an architectural analogy, not an evidence-based recommendation

- **Severity:** high
- **Specific memo passage attacked:** "**Build a DV Crisis Management System.** Port the CMS architecture — precinct-targeted CVI sites, 24/7 responder teams, hospital-based intervention, John Jay REC outcome evaluation — onto domestic and household violence."
- **Why this is a problem:** The CMS / Cure Violence model is built around credible-messenger street outreach for retaliatory gun violence between rival groups in public space. Domestic violence is intra-household, private, repeat-victimization-driven, and frequently un-reported until escalation. The mechanism of action — interrupters who can mediate between gang-affiliated young men in a neighborhood — has no obvious analog inside a household. The memo's own "supported / not supported" table honestly labels this "hypothesis to verify," yet the headline thesis and the dollar figure both treat it as proven. Calling it "by analogy" is intellectual honesty in the appendix; it does not survive being the central recommendation. A hiring manager reading this will ask: name one city that has done this and shown a result. The memo cannot.
- **Suggested research to confirm or deny:** Determine whether any U.S. or international jurisdiction has piloted a CVI-style credible-messenger intervention against intimate-partner / household violence specifically, and what the published outcome data show — and separately, examine whether the criminology literature on DV recidivism reduction (batterer-intervention programs, lethality-assessment protocols, GPS monitoring, housing-first for survivors) ranks the CVI architecture above existing DV-specific evidence-based interventions.

## Critique 5: The $40–60M cost figure is back-of-envelope dressed up as analysis

- **Severity:** medium
- **Specific memo passage attacked:** "$40-60M annually at scale, derived from CMS's ~$86M / 29-precinct footprint and a comparable scope ambition for DV (target: top 25-30 DV-incidence precincts)."
- **Why this is a problem:** This is a per-precinct unit cost ($86M / 29 ≈ $3M) multiplied by a precinct count chosen to land in a politically palatable range. It assumes (a) DV-CMS unit economics match shooting-CMS unit economics, (b) hospital-responder co-location at H+H costs nothing additional, (c) credible-messenger labor supply for DV exists at the same wage point as for shooting interruption, and (d) the "top 25–30 DV-incidence precincts" identification is even possible given the author's own admission that pd_cd 109 cannot be disaggregated. None of these assumptions is examined. A budget director would push back inside thirty seconds.
- **Suggested research to confirm or deny:** Test whether NYC ONS / Comptroller cost detail on CMS shows fixed vs variable cost by precinct, and whether the marginal precinct-add cost (rather than the average) is the right unit for projecting a DV-CMS budget — and whether CMS's actual per-precinct costs vary by an order of magnitude across the 29 sites.

## Critique 6: The B-HEARD scaling claim leans on a misread of the Comptroller audit

- **Severity:** medium
- **Specific memo passage attacked:** "Comptroller Lander's 2024 audit found that **35% of even the eligible calls in pilot precincts** didn't get a B-HEARD team, with reasons untracked. Scaling to 77 precincts and closing the unserved-eligible gap is mechanical: more teams, dispatcher protocol changes, accountability metrics."
- **Why this is a problem:** "Mechanical" is the load-bearing word and it's wrong. The audit's finding is that 35% of *eligible* calls during *operating hours within pilot precincts* still got NYPD response — and the audit specifically said reasons were untracked, which means we don't know whether the gap is dispatcher behavior, team availability, call-classification disagreement, or refusal at scene. Treating an unexplained gap as "mechanical to close" is exactly the kind of consultant move that produces failed implementations. The 28 → 77 tripling math also ignores that B-HEARD teams operate limited hours and that the eligible-call rate per precinct is non-uniform; "approximately 3× current scope" is not a staffing model.
- **Suggested research to confirm or deny:** Determine whether the Comptroller audit, or subsequent FDNY/H+H operational reviews, identify the specific causes of the 35% unserved-eligible gap, and whether those causes are addressable through additional teams (a supply problem) versus dispatcher protocol or eligibility-coding issues (a routing problem) — these have very different cost and timeline implications.

## Critique 7: The Minto SCQ does not actually answer the Question it poses

- **Severity:** medium
- **Specific memo passage attacked:** "**What should NYC actually do?** … **Build a DV Crisis Management System.** … This package targets the actual driver of NYC's structural assault rise…"
- **Why this is a problem:** The Question framed is "what should NYC actually do?" but the Situation/Complication setup is about a *measurement and framing* failure ("the gap is invisible in current public reporting … no NYC public communication has ever named the 14-year structural assault rise"). The thesis answers a different question — "what intervention should NYC fund?" — and then awkwardly demotes the measurement story to a secondary observation. Either the memo is about a reporting/framing failure (in which case the recommendation should be about reporting), or it is about a substantive intervention gap (in which case the framing critique is window dressing). Trying to be both makes the pyramid wobble: the three supports are about programs, but the Complication is about visibility. A reviewer steel-manning the counter says: "If the rise has been visible in Vital City, Gothamist, and the Comptroller's own work, the framing problem is overstated, and your central thesis is left standing on the DV-CMS analogy alone."
- **Suggested research to confirm or deny:** Test whether the structural felony-assault rise has in fact been named in city-affiliated reporting (City Council oversight hearings, Comptroller bulletins, ENDGBV annual reports, MOCJ briefings) over 2019–2024 — i.e., is the "invisible to institutional reporting" claim empirically true, or is it true only of the Mayor's annual press release.

## Critique 8: Selective citation of intervention evidence — the strongest-evidence interventions are demoted to a footnote

- **Severity:** medium
- **Specific memo passage attacked:** "The DV-CMS proposal combines models 4 (HVIP-style hospital response) and the CMS-specific architecture; B-HEARD scaling implements model 5; CBT (model 3) is a possible add-on for the at-risk-youth slice but not the headline action."
- **Why this is a problem:** The memo's own evidence ranking puts hot-spots policing (#1, 29% assault reduction, Braga Campbell) and focused deterrence (#2) above HVIPs (#4, "underpowered RCTs") and CAHOOTS-style response (#5, "indirect" effects on assault). Yet the recommendation is built on #4 and #5, not #1 and #2. The author handwaves this with "weaker fit for DV-driven felony assault" for focused deterrence and silently drops hot-spots policing entirely from the recommendation set. A skeptical reader notices that the recommendation tracks the politically palatable interventions, not the strongest-evidence ones, and the memo never defends the choice. This is exactly the failure mode where an analyst's priors drive the recommendation and the evidence base is selectively marshaled to dress it.
- **Suggested research to confirm or deny:** Determine whether hot-spots policing with problem-oriented design has been evaluated specifically against DV / household assault (rather than only street and firearm crime), and whether the Braga meta-analytic effect generalizes or attenuates in that setting — this is the missing comparator the memo silently skips.
