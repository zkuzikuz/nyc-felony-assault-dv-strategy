# Round 2 Critiques — Methodological Peer Reviewer

## Critique 1: The Maryland LAP "35-45% female-homicide reduction" effect size is treated as a deployment-grade point estimate without disclosing the identification strategy or uncertainty bounds

- **Severity:** high
- **Specific memo passage attacked:** "Lethality Assessment Programs (35-45% female-homicide reduction in Maryland; CDC-rated 'supported intervention')" (TL;DR; restated identically in §3 and audit table).
- **Methodological failure:** The Messing et al. Maryland LAP work is a non-randomized field study comparing intervention to comparison sites, with self-selected participating agencies and largely self-reported re-assault outcomes — not a population-level intimate-partner-homicide RCT. The memo cites a single point-estimate range with no confidence interval, no description of identification (DiD? matched comparison? simple pre/post?), and no acknowledgment that female-IPH is a low-base-rate outcome where small numerator changes flip 35-45% effects in either direction. NIJ's "supported intervention" rating is a meaningfully weaker bar than Campbell/Cochrane "effective." Citing a quasi-experimental effect at deployment grade — without identification strategy, CI, or power discussion — is the load-bearing methodological failure for Support 1.
- **Suggested research to confirm or deny:** Test whether the underlying Maryland LAP evaluations report a confidence interval and identification strategy for the female-homicide endpoint specifically; determine whether female-IPH was a pre-registered primary endpoint or a downstream/secondary outcome; verify what NIJ "supported" means relative to Campbell/Cochrane "effective" criteria.

## Critique 2: The High Point OFDVI 20% effect comes from a single-site pre/post study with no formal counterfactual, and the Sweden replication is not statistically independent

- **Severity:** high
- **Specific memo passage attacked:** "High Point Offender-Focused Domestic Violence Initiative, ~20% reduction in IPDV calls and arrests, replicated in Sweden's Malmö program" (§3).
- **Methodological failure:** Sechrist & Weil's High Point study is a single-jurisdiction pre/post observational design (~100K-population mid-South city). No synthetic control, no matched-comparison jurisdiction, no DiD — so the 20% is exposed to citywide-trend confounding (national IPV declined over the same window), agency-specific reporting changes (the program almost certainly altered intake), and regression to the mean (OFDVI-adopting sites are typically high-incidence). The Malmö "replication" is a separate quasi-experiment in a different legal/welfare context; treating two correlated single-site quasi-experiments as cumulative overstates statistical independence.
- **Suggested research to confirm or deny:** Determine whether the Sechrist & Weil OFDVI evaluation, or any subsequent NNSC-affiliated evaluation, used a counterfactual identification strategy (DiD, synthetic control, RCT, RDD) versus simple pre/post comparison, and whether the 20% reduction survives a comparison against contemporaneous national or state-level IPV trends.

## Critique 3: Multiple-comparisons exposure across the analytic pipeline is unaccounted for, and the silhouette = 0.44 result has not been tested for offense-set sensitivity

- **Severity:** medium
- **Specific memo passage attacked:** "clustering all 16 top felony categories on their 2017-2024 trajectories yields three robust clusters (silhouette = 0.44 at k=3 … bootstrap re-clustering confirms the partition is stable for ≥75% of categories)."
- **Methodological failure:** R1 addressed k-sweep and year-bootstrap stability, but neither tested **leave-one-offense-out** sensitivity — the most relevant perturbation, given that the offense set was already filtered ("top 16," excluding two reclassification artifacts). The silhouette is a maximum over k=2..7, an implicit multiplicity-inflated statistic; 0.44 is the best of six attempts, uncorrected. More broadly, the memo runs at least four implicit hypothesis families (cluster partition, pre-COVID structural classification with a hand-tuned 5%/10%/18% rule, pd_cd 109 sub-category attribution, per-precinct spatial decomposition) without any family-wise error budget. Several "strong" audit-table rows are correlated tests of the same signal.
- **Suggested research to confirm or deny:** Determine whether the silhouette = 0.44 / k = 3 result survives leave-one-offense-out re-clustering across all 16 offenses (does dropping any single offense flip the global maximum to a different k or reduce silhouette below 0.30?) and report a family-wise error budget for the four hypothesis families the memo tests.

## Critique 4: External validity from Maryland and High Point to NYC is asserted, not argued — moderator-mismatch attenuation should be the prior, not the exception

- **Severity:** high
- **Specific memo passage attacked:** "LAP / OFDVI evaluations from Maryland and High Point will translate to NYC at scale … external evidence (the rec is a deployment bet, not a research bet)" (audit table).
- **Methodological failure:** Maryland LAP was tested across small/mid-sized jurisdictions in a single state with a particular court structure and demographic composition. High Point is a 100K-population mid-South city where police, courts, advocates, and probation fit in one room — a defining operational characteristic of focused-deterrence call-ins. NYC has 8.3M residents, 77 precincts, a five-borough DA structure, and case volumes orders of magnitude larger. The transportability literature (Tipton 2014; Stuart/Cole) treats moderator-mismatch as the *typical* reason effects attenuate — often by 50%+. The memo provides no covariate-overlap analysis, no moderator-balance discussion, and no acknowledgment that NYC may sit outside the support of the source-population distribution. "Deployment bet, not research bet" assumes external validity holds; the transportability literature would frame that as the hypothesis to test.
- **Suggested research to confirm or deny:** Test whether jurisdictions of NYC's scale (1M+ population, multi-DA structure) appear in any LAP or OFDVI evaluation pool, and whether the Tipton/Stuart transportability framework provides defensible projections of Maryland or High Point effects onto NYC's covariate profile — including quantified moderator-mismatch attenuation for similar focused-deterrence ports.

## Critique 5: The cited effect sizes (35-45%, 20%, 21%, 35%) do not specify their denominators or treated population, making citywide projection arithmetically undefined

- **Severity:** high
- **Specific memo passage attacked:** "(35-45% female-homicide reduction in Maryland; CDC-rated 'supported intervention') … (~20% reduction, replicated in Sweden) … 21% reduction in shootings (Comptroller Lander 2024) … 35% of *eligible* calls in pilot precincts didn't get a B-HEARD team" (TL;DR + audit table).
- **Methodological failure:** The "35-45%" LAP figure could refer to (a) per-program-participant homicide-victimization risk reduction over follow-up, (b) jurisdiction-level female-IPH rate change, or (c) some intermediate. "20%" OFDVI could refer to program-eligible-offender recidivism, citywide IPDV call/arrest rates, or call rates in target areas only. These produce wildly different citywide projections: a 35% per-participant homicide reduction reaching 5% of NYC's DV population is not arithmetically the same as a 35% citywide rate change. Deploying LAP "citywide" implicitly assumes the Maryland effect translates to a population-level rate, but the underlying study almost certainly measured a per-treated-population effect. The CMS 21% (treatment-area vs citywide) and B-HEARD 35% (denominator = eligible calls during operating hours within pilot precincts) have the same problem.
- **Suggested research to confirm or deny:** For each cited effect size (LAP 35-45%, OFDVI 20%, CMS 21%, B-HEARD 35%), determine the exact numerator and denominator in the source study, and re-derive what proportion of NYC's 29,500 annual felony assaults the treated population could plausibly cover — producing scope-adjusted projections with explicit uncertainty rather than face-value point estimates.

## Critique 6: The memo applies an asymmetric standard of evidence — "tested elsewhere ≈ deployable in NYC" for LAP/OFDVI, but "untested in NYC ≈ disqualifying" for CVI-for-DV

- **Severity:** medium
- **Specific memo passage attacked:** "no published evaluation of CVI-style credible-messenger work targeting DV exists … CMS-for-DV ranks at #5 — untested … LAP and High Point OFDVI are the strongest-evidence DV-recidivism interventions" (TL;DR and §3).
- **Methodological failure:** Both LAP and OFDVI are also untested in NYC; the audit table concedes "external evidence (the rec is a deployment bet)." The chain "X was evaluated in jurisdiction A; therefore X is deployable in NYC" is the same chain rejected for CVI-for-DV under "no DV-specific evaluation exists." Both are ports across different conditions (jurisdiction → NYC for LAP; outcome class → DV for CVI). The clean framing would either (a) treat both as untested in NYC and rank by source-study quality plus mechanism plausibility, or (b) reject both as transportability bets. Choosing (a) for LAP/OFDVI and (b) for CVI privileges domain-of-evaluation similarity over jurisdiction-of-evaluation similarity — a defensible prior, but one the memo does not defend.
- **Suggested research to confirm or deny:** Determine whether the program-evaluation literature explicitly addresses the choice between cross-outcome-class generalization (CMS shooting → CMS DV) and cross-jurisdiction generalization (Maryland LAP → NYC LAP), and whether either is reliably the smaller bet — or whether the choice is necessarily case-specific and must be argued, not asserted.

## Critique 7: The CMS "21% shooting reduction" used as the implicit evidence benchmark is itself observational, and its identification has not been interrogated

- **Severity:** medium
- **Specific memo passage attacked:** "Crisis Management System / Cure Violence … 21% reduction in shootings (Comptroller Lander 2024) … Proven model — for *gun violence*" (audit table); serendipity-finding #1 uses CMS-for-shootings as the gold-standard comparator CVI-for-DV fails to match.
- **Methodological failure:** The Comptroller Lander 2024 "Cure for Crisis" report is a city-agency analysis using before/after and treated/untreated precinct comparisons — not a design with a credible counterfactual (synthetic control, DiD with pretrend tests, RDD). Calling CMS a "proven model" while ranking CVI-for-DV as "untested" applies different evidentiary standards: the proof for CMS is a city-agency report, while the bar for CVI-for-DV is a published evaluation. If peer-reviewed quasi-experiment is the standard, CMS-for-shootings should be flagged as weak identification; if city-agency analysis suffices, CVI-for-DV is evaluable on similar terms. The DV-specific ranking is normalized against a comparator whose identification has not been audited.
- **Suggested research to confirm or deny:** Determine whether the Comptroller Lander 2024 CMS evaluation, or the John Jay REC 2017 S. Bronx / E. NY evaluations, used credible identification (synthetic control, DiD with pretrend tests, RDD) versus simple before/after or treated/untreated comparisons, and whether the 21% / 37-50% figures survive sensitivity to alternative comparator selection or pretrend violation.

## Critique 8: The "35% gap is primarily a supply problem" framing for B-HEARD rests on numbers the audit itself does not reconcile

- **Severity:** medium
- **Specific memo passage attacked:** "Round 1 research on the cause: **primarily a supply problem** — the largest single component is that B-HEARD does not operate during overnight hours (1am-9am), and over 14,000 unserved eligible calls came in during those hours" (§2).
- **Methodological failure:** R1 (Q6) flagged but the memo dropped the underlying inconsistency: "Of 13,042 unserved eligible calls, 'over 14,000 calls determined to be eligible' came in during overnight hours" — the overnight count *exceeds the total unserved gap*. Either (a) eligibility coding includes windows where B-HEARD coverage was structurally impossible (the 35% is then a definitional artifact, not a gap), or (b) the counts use non-comparable denominators. The supply-vs-routing decomposition cannot be derived from numbers that don't sum. The memo then projects a $30-50M cost on the supply-problem framing without acknowledging this.
- **Suggested research to confirm or deny:** Determine whether the Comptroller B-HEARD audit reports a reconciled denominator for unserved eligible calls (i.e., totals that sum) and whether the 14K overnight figure and 13K total-unserved figure use the same eligibility definition; re-derive the supply-vs-routing decomposition only from a reconciled denominator, with explicit uncertainty for the unreconciled residual.
