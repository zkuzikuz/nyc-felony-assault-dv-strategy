# Round 3 Critiques — Hostile Policy Critic
**Adopted posture:** DV-survivor advocate and coalition organizer with a secondary civil-liberties lens. We are the people whose lives the memo claims to be saving, and we say the memo's recommendation is a police-led screening-and-coercion package wearing a survivor-services costume — funded by displacement of programs that already keep women alive, run by exactly the institutions DV survivors most distrust, and evaluated against the wrong outcome.

---

## Critique 1: LAP is administered by police at the scene of an arrest. Survivors who screen "low-risk" get triaged out of services they would otherwise get.

- **Severity:** high
- **Specific memo passage attacked:** "Adopt LAP as the standard NYPD/H+H response protocol in 4-6 high-DV-incidence precincts. The 11-question lethality screen administered at scene, high-risk cases auto-referred to a 24/7 advocate hotline + safety planning + supportive-housing access."
- **Political/constituency/implementation failure:** The memo treats LAP as a one-way ratchet — high-risk gets referred, low-risk gets ordinary policing. But once an "evidence-based" 11-question instrument is the gateway to advocate handoff and housing access, a "low-risk" score becomes a denial-of-service. NYC's DV hotlines, family justice centers, and supportive-housing slots are already rationed; a police-administered screen that sorts survivors into "high-risk gets the hotline" creates a bureaucratic instrument that screens women OUT of services they currently receive on self-identification. The Maryland Koppa effect was estimated against jurisdictions with no prior survivor-services infrastructure; bolting LAP onto NYC's existing ENDGBV / family-justice-center system without rules against using the score as a rationing key risks net-negative service access for the moderate-risk majority. The memo never specifies whether the score will be used only as an enhancer of services or also as a gate. That is a survivor-coalition deal-breaker in any City Council hearing.
- **Suggested research to confirm or deny:** Determine whether jurisdictions that adopted LAP as standard police protocol subsequently reduced (or expanded) advocate-referral and shelter-bed access for self-identified survivors who scored below the lethality cutoff, relative to pre-LAP baselines.

## Critique 2: OFDVI's "call-in + sanctions ladder" is a coercive surveillance program. The memo never acknowledges what that means in NYC.

- **Severity:** high
- **Specific memo passage attacked:** "Launch in 4-6 high-DV precincts: identify chronic high-risk DV offenders, 'call-in' notifications with sanctions ladders, coordinated response across police / probation / courts / DV advocates."
- **Political/constituency/implementation failure:** Strip the criminology jargon and what the memo is recommending is: NYPD identifies a roster of mostly Black and brown men, summons them to a room at 1 Police Plaza or a precinct, and tells them they are now under coordinated multi-agency surveillance (police + probation + courts) for a category of conduct that has not yet been adjudicated. The "high-risk" designation is generated by police-held data on prior contacts — i.e. arrests and complaints, not convictions. NYC has lived through stop-and-frisk, gang databases, and the youth-justice scandals around named-list policing; the gang-database lawsuits (Black & Latino Caucus 2018; Bronx Defenders litigation) make precisely this kind of pre-conviction roster legally and politically toxic. The memo cites NNSC partnership as an asset; it does not acknowledge that NNSC's signature mechanism is the call-in, and the call-in's civil-liberties profile in NYC's specific surveillance history is the load-bearing political question. Any community-board hearing on this lands here within ten minutes.
- **Suggested research to confirm or deny:** Determine the demographic composition of OFDVI call-in lists in jurisdictions where rosters have been published or FOIA'd, the false-positive rate (proportion of called-in individuals who had no qualifying conduct in the post-call-in observation window), and whether litigation analogous to NYC's gang-database suits has been filed against OFDVI rosters.

## Critique 3: "Pilot in 4-6 high-incidence precincts" sites the program in the same Black and Latino neighborhoods that already bear the heaviest enforcement footprint.

- **Severity:** high
- **Specific memo passage attacked:** "Pilot deployments — not citywide rollouts — with rigorous evaluation as the primary Phase-1 deliverable: LAP pilot: Adopt LAP as the standard NYPD/H+H response protocol in 4-6 high-DV-incidence precincts."
- **Political/constituency/implementation failure:** "High-DV-incidence precincts" in NYC overwhelmingly map to East New York, Brownsville, the South Bronx, central Harlem, and parts of Jamaica — the same precinct list that has carried disproportionate stop-and-frisk volume, the original Cure Violence sites, and the most aggressive subway and quality-of-life enforcement. Stacking LAP + OFDVI onto the CMS footprint concentrates new police-administered screening and surveillance instruments in neighborhoods already saturated with them, while leaving DV survivors in lower-incidence-but-equally-victimized places (large parts of Queens, Staten Island, the Upper East Side, the Upper West Side — where DV is also chronic but less reported) with nothing. The memo presents precinct selection as a research-design optimization (statistical power, matched comparators); the political reality is that the geography of the pilot is the geography of who gets surveilled. A community-organization advocate from a non-CMS precinct will read this as: the city has decided to double-down on the same neighborhoods rather than build something in mine.
- **Suggested research to confirm or deny:** Determine the overlap between the proposed top-DV-incidence precincts and the existing CMS / Cure Violence / Subway-Safety-Surge footprints; whether DV reporting rates per estimated victimization differ across higher- and lower-reporting neighborhoods (i.e., is "incidence" a measure of where DV happens or where DV gets reported); and what fraction of NYC's DV-survivor population would be in vs out of the 4-6 pilot precinct catchment.

## Critique 4: $40-80M of new spending against the FY26 fiscal-cliff backdrop will be paid for by cutting community-based DV services. The memo does not say this.

- **Severity:** high
- **Specific memo passage attacked:** "$3-8M/year to pilot LAP in 4-6 precincts; $2-5M to launch and run an OFDVI in the same set; evaluation costs run another $1-2M per program… 24-hour operations + tripling precinct coverage is roughly $30-50M annually beyond current B-HEARD budget."
- **Political/constituency/implementation failure:** The package totals roughly $40-65M in new annual spend, plus operationalizing a $650M housing pipeline. NYC is heading into a multi-year fiscal contraction — federal pandemic dollars expired, the migrant-services line is consuming reserves, and DCAS and DSS are already running PEG cuts. New money for two pilots and B-HEARD scaling will not appear from nothing; it will displace existing line items. The most natural displacement targets, given the package's framing, are exactly the community-based DV programs the memo claims to be augmenting: the family-justice-center contracts, the DV hotline, ENDGBV's small-dollar grants to community groups, and the legal-services nonprofits that currently serve survivors who cannot or will not call police. The memo's silence on funding source is not neutral — it is what permits the opposition to assume the worst, because the worst is the path of least resistance for OMB. If the memo cannot name a non-DV-services funding source, advocates will treat it as a transfer FROM survivor services TO police-administered screening.
- **Suggested research to confirm or deny:** Determine which existing FY25/FY26 NYC budget lines are mathematically capable of absorbing $40-65M in new annual spending without further federal aid (NYPD, DSS, ACS, DOHMH, ENDGBV, MOCMH); and whether any prior CMS / B-HEARD scale-ups in recent budget cycles were paid for in part by reductions in adjacent community-based service contracts.

## Critique 5: NNSC and the existing CMS vendor list capture the contract pipeline; new entrants are excluded by design.

- **Severity:** medium
- **Specific memo passage attacked:** "Partner with John Jay's NNSC (which ran NYC Ceasefire since 2014 and managed High Point's original OFDVI)… NNSC's NYC infrastructure is the natural launchpad for an OFDVI."
- **Political/constituency/implementation failure:** The memo names a single technical-assistance partner (NNSC) and quietly frames the supportive infrastructure as "existing CMS vendors" — i.e., the 24-vendor cumulative-payments table from the Comptroller's CMS report. Sole-source designation by analytic recommendation is a procurement pattern: by the time RFPs go out, the architecture has already been written around one TA partner's methodology and one vendor pool's capabilities. Survivor-led organizations that have built credibility in specific NYC immigrant, LGBTQ, and disabled-survivor communities — Sakhi, Womankind, Violence Intervention Program, Anti-Violence Project, Barrier Free Living — are not in the CMS vendor pool and will not be in the OFDVI vendor pool unless explicitly carved in. The memo does not acknowledge this. Recommending an architecture is also recommending an incumbency.
- **Suggested research to confirm or deny:** Determine whether NYC procurements that follow a named technical-assistance partner ("partner with NNSC") subsequently award implementation contracts disproportionately to that partner's existing vendor network, relative to procurements without a named TA partner; and what fraction of NYC's culturally specific DV-survivor-services organizations currently hold city contracts of comparable scale.

## Critique 6: "Domestic violence is the dominant driver" is a frame that legitimizes expanded enforcement. The same data permits a housing-and-poverty frame the memo never considers.

- **Severity:** high
- **Specific memo passage attacked:** "NYC has a measured, multi-decade structural rise in felony assault driven primarily by domestic violence… NYC has not deployed [DV-specific evidence-based interventions] at scale. What should NYC actually do?"
- **Political/constituency/implementation failure:** The memo's diagnostic move — "the rise is DV-driven, therefore the response is DV-specific intervention" — sounds neutral but is an enforcement frame. The same 14-year structural rise correlates tightly with: NYC's loss of 700+ inpatient psychiatric beds; the collapse of affordable rental supply (median rent rose ~50% over the same window); a documented rise in household crowding among low-income tenants; the 2014-2024 erosion of voluntary-departure shelter access for survivors; the SNAP/TANF benefit cliffs created by 2018-2022 federal changes; and the post-2014 contraction of legal-services funding for tenants. A progressive housing-and-mental-health frame on the same data would land on: build supportive housing, restore psychiatric beds, fund tenant attorneys, expand SNAP, do what Bridge to Home is supposed to do at scale — and quietly defund the police-administered screening overlay because survivors who can leave do not need a lethality assessment to do it. The memo demotes the housing piece to "Support 3" behind two enforcement-adjacent pilots; the political question is why. The memo's ranking is a choice, not a fact, and the choice serves NYPD's interests over the housing-justice coalition's. If the housing fix is the substantive lever, leading with two policing pilots wastes political capital and consolidates NYPD's claim to the DV problem.
- **Suggested research to confirm or deny:** Determine whether the 2010-2024 NYC felony-assault rise correlates more strongly with DV-coded incidents specifically or with household-crowding, eviction-filing, and supportive-housing-bed metrics over the same window; and whether jurisdictions that responded to rising DV with housing-and-services investment (rather than police-administered screening) showed comparable or larger reductions in DV outcomes.

## Critique 7: The memo says nothing about immigrant survivors, LGBTQ survivors, or survivors with disabilities. Police-administered screening is documented to fail them.

- **Severity:** medium
- **Specific memo passage attacked:** "the 11-question lethality screen administered at scene, high-risk cases auto-referred to a 24/7 advocate hotline + safety planning + supportive-housing access."
- **Political/constituency/implementation failure:** The Lethality Assessment instrument was developed and validated in jurisdictions whose survivor populations are demographically narrower than NYC's. The 11 questions assume a male-perpetrator / female-victim dyad and English fluency, and the in-scene administration assumes the survivor is willing to disclose to a uniformed officer at the moment of greatest fear of retaliation. NYC's survivor population includes large communities of undocumented women (for whom police contact triggers immigration-enforcement risk), trans and nonbinary survivors (whom the screen's gendered framing misclassifies), and Deaf/disabled survivors (for whom the screen's verbal administration is inaccessible without trained interpreters not deployed at scene). The memo's silence on these populations is not benign — it tells coalition partners that the recommendation was written without them at the table. Sanctuary-policy advocates will read this as: the recommendation deepens NYPD-as-frontline-DV-responder at the precise moment the city should be building survivor-led pathways that don't start with a 911 call.
- **Suggested research to confirm or deny:** Determine the demographic profile of the Maryland LAP evaluation sample relative to NYC's DV-survivor population (immigration status, primary language, gender identity, disability status); and whether jurisdictions with high undocumented-immigrant populations report differential LAP-completion or LAP-referral rates compared to the source-study jurisdictions.

## Critique 8: The memo's evaluation-built-in framing is unfalsifiable in practice — pilots that "show no effect" still keep getting funded.

- **Severity:** medium
- **Specific memo passage attacked:** "Pilots succeed if they show statistically significant effects under transparent identification; recommendation is to scale only what does."
- **Political/constituency/implementation failure:** The memo presents Phase-1 evaluation as a hard kill-switch: pilots that don't work get cut. NYC's actual track record on evaluation-and-scale is the opposite — Subway Safety Surge, the CRT, the original B-HEARD pilot, even CMS itself in its early years all continued and grew without published causal evaluation, and political pressure protects programs once advocates and vendors are mobilized around them. Once 4-6 precincts have LAP and OFDVI and a vendor pool has hired, the political cost of a "your pilot didn't work, we're cutting it" decision is borne by the City Council member whose district hosts the pilot, not by the analyst who wrote the memo. The "evaluation-as-deliverable" framing is therefore politically naive: it presumes a future political environment willing to terminate a survivor-services program on a p-value, which has never happened in NYC for any program reaching this scale. A budget hawk and a survivor advocate end up in the same place: the kill-switch is a fiction, the spending is permanent, and the evaluation rigor was the cover story.
- **Suggested research to confirm or deny:** Determine, for NYC public-safety and human-services pilots launched 2010-2024 with announced "rigorous evaluation" components (CMS, B-HEARD, NeON Arts, Cure Violence Bronx expansions, ATIs), what fraction were terminated or de-scaled following negative or null evaluation findings, vs what fraction continued or expanded irrespective of the evaluation outcome.
